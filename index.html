    KINO

    WHAT IS KINO?

    KINO is an archive of film formats that have emerged throughout over a century of cinema's history. It brings together the historical context and characteristics of each format, along with the distinct images produced by films shot in them. These platforms are not just specifications of cameras or the size of a theater screen. Each format generates a different kind of image through its own texture, resolution, and frame. This goes beyond technical difference; it reshapes the very way we perceive cinema. Texture, grain, resolution, frame size are not simply technical elements, but a language that constructs the image of film. Cinema is not only defined by what it shows, but by how it is captured. KINO documents film not in terms of what is shown, but how it is shown. 16mm, 35mm, 70mm, VistaVision and IMAX. Each format carries its own history and way of seeing.

    INTERVIEWEE FILM DIRECTOR & PROFESSOR MOLLY SHEARS
    INTERVIEW WITH MOLLY SHEARS TORONTO, ON

    FILM IS STILL ALIVE

    Molly Shears is a writer and director based in Toronto. She received her BFA in fine arts from OCAD University (CA) in 2016 and her MFA in filmmaking from The London Film School (UK) in 2020. As of 2024, Molly teaches in the Faculty of Art at OCAD University. She is currently in development for her first feature film as well as several short-form projects. KINO asked her insights about contemporary filmmaking environment and the analog film format.

    I understand you have experience with both film and digital formats. Could you share your perspective on the primary differences in the "shooting experience" between the two?

    I have used both digital and film formats many times. When I was in film school, we were trained on film with minimal amounts of footage so that were forced to be more prepared in our shot lists. Though this made me more disciplined, ultimately, I think I had to unlearn this process because it didn't highlight the importance of doing multiple takes until you have "the one." This all ties into the difference between the process of digital/film. With digital, it is exponentially cheaper to keep shooting, and to shoot more, so you have enough to work with in your edit. In film, this simply becomes more expensive. When shooting on film, you also don't get playback, which requires a confidence in yourself and your team that you got the shot.

    Cinema is visual storytelling. Elements such as specific aspect ratios, textures, and layout specifications play a crucial role in conveying a narrative. How do you believe the choice of format influences the storytelling process?

    The choice of format absolutely influences the storytelling process! It's not the only visual factor, of course, but it is a great visual storytelling tool. I just shot a film set in the late 90s/early 2000s, and the 16mm texture really helped sell the time period, in addition to the production design and costuming. In many cases, film make the film feel more alive (in my opinion, especially if it's grainy).

    As cinematic history continues, digital cameras have emerged as the industry standard, surpassing film. What are your thoughts on this technological shift?

    Unfortunately, I think this technological shift is inevitable because of budget constraints across all of the film industry. Don't get me wrong; I think digital is a really important tool and has made the art form much more accessible to creators who probably wouldn't have had the opportunity to become filmmakers otherwise. What I hope for is the continued access to both film and digital, and for there to be a continued push to keep film alive.

    With the rise of digital, many prominent directors who started in the film era—such as Michael Mann, Kevin Smith, David Fincher, and Robert Rodriguez—have transitioned to digital workflows. How do you think this has affected the "visual integrity" and aesthetic finish of their films?

    I don't believe that any filmmaker loses visual integrity by switching to digital. I am no expert on any of those directors, but digital opens up a lot of doors in terms of visual effects and post-production techniques.

    On the contrary, directors such as Christopher Nolan, Paul Thomas Anderson, and Quentin Tarantino steadfastly commit to shooting on film, even in the digital age. What is your take on this creative persistence? Furthermore, what are your thoughts on the visual fidelity and character of their film-based works?

    All of those directors are massive film buffs who also have massive budgets on their films. They write and create films in which the use of film lends itself well to the overall aesthetic and texture of the movie. Again, I just think the use of film or digital depends on the project. Film is great for some projects. Digital is great for other projects. It's great that we have both.

    Some directors express a strong disdain for digital. Quentin Tarantino famously stated he would never shoot on digital, describing it as "the death of cinema" (or "the shits"). Do you agree or disagree with this provocative assessment? What is the reasoning behind your stance?

    Quentin Tarantino loves to make a provocative statement! Would I want to see a digital Tarantino film? Of course not. But do I want other creators to make digital films that are just as good as his films? Of course. That's their choice for their films and their stories.

    The 2020s have seen a significant number of Hollywood releases shot on film. The Brutalist, Bugonia, and One Battle After Another were shot on VistaVision; Sinners on 65mm; Marty Supreme and Babylon on 35mm; and The Smashing Machine on 16mm. Do you believe this diverse range of format choices contributes positively to the visual diversity of modern cinema?

    Absolutely, I want to see a diverse range of films in the cinema, and technological specs are part of that.

    In the case of VistaVision, it took over 60 years—from 1961’s One-Eyed Jacks to 2024’s The Brutalist—for it to be utilized as a primary format again. Why do you think Hollywood is returning to this half-century-old format now? Do you view this "renaissance" of legacy formats as a positive direction?

    VistaVision has been used within those 60 years, but definitely decreased over time. I'm all for VistaVision to have a come back, I'm not sure that it will in any massive way because its so much more expensive than regular film.

    Directors like Damien Chazelle ("Bablyon"), Josh ("Marty Supreme") and Benny Safdie ("The Smashing Machine"), Ryan Coogler ("Sinners"), and Brady Corbet ("The Brutalist") are all "digital-native" filmmakers born in the mid-to-late 80s. Despite this, they favor film. Why do you think this younger generation is turning back to analog?

    The younger generation is definitely turning back to analog. When all of the possibilities of digital emerged, it seems to me that it made film sort out of fashion. But the younger generation feels a nostalgia and appreciation for film (myself included) and I see many filmmakers prefer it over digital.

    Today, film production has decreased, and costs have skyrocketed. Some argue that shooting on these specialized formats is a "waste of time and money." However, many still fight for the medium. Do you believe film production should be encouraged more broadly, or do you feel otherwise? What is the core reason for your opinion?

    I'm not sure that film production has decreased, in fact I think it has increased in a major way because it is way more accessible of an artistic format. In terms of encouragement, as a university professor I don't believe you can push an artist to do anything different than what they want to do, but you can give them the tools they need to explore.
